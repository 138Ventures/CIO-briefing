## 2. Macro Regime

### Growth: Decelerating Under Supply-Shock Pressure

The macro picture flipped in March. What was a consensus "soft landing / no landing" narrative three months ago is now a stagflation debate. The Iran war — with Brent crude above $110 and Pentagon reports of ground troop preparations — has injected genuine supply-shock uncertainty.

- **US GDP:** Goldman still forecasts 2.7% for 2026, but this predates the March oil spike. PIMCO had already flagged below-trend growth from tariffs and immigration tightening. BofA FMS shows 51% of managers now expect "below-trend growth + above-trend inflation" — the textbook definition of stagflation.
- **Global:** JPM assigns 35% recession probability. Vanguard sees the first half as softer, with potential re-acceleration in H2 if geopolitics stabilize. Morgan Stanley is the outlier, arguing this is early-cycle re-acceleration (out of consensus).

### Inflation: Re-Accelerating From Energy Shock

- CPI held at 2.4% in February, but PPI surged to 3.4% — the hottest since February 2025.
- EY estimates March CPI could hit 3.3% as gasoline prices jump ~15%.
- The BofA FMS inflation expectations indicator surged from net +9% to net +45% in a single month.
- PIMCO flags tariff passthrough as an additional inflation risk on top of energy.

### Central Bank Stance: Hawkish Hold

- **Fed:** Held at 3.50-3.75% on March 18. Dot plot still shows one cut for 2026 but with "elevated upside risks to inflation." Market pricing has collapsed — June cut probability dropped to 18.4%, September to 43.6%. December at 60.5%.
- **Implied year-end rate:** 3.43% (futures), vs. current 3.64%. Markets now pricing ~25bps of cuts total for 2026.
- PIMCO notes most major central banks (ex-BoJ) are approaching neutral by end-2026. BoJ rate hike remains a tail risk.

### Liquidity: Tightening

- Cash levels surging (BofA FMS: 4.3%, largest monthly jump since March 2020).
- Dollar strengthening (DXY 99.4) — tightens global dollar liquidity.
- Credit conditions deteriorating: 46% of FMS respondents say default risks are above normal (up from 17% prior month).
- 63% identify private credit as the most likely source of a systemic credit event — highest ever recorded.
- BIS flags Synthetic Risk Transfers (SRTs) — bank risk offloaded to non-banks has grown fivefold since 2016, covering ~EUR 800B in loans. Opaque and fragmented.
- Lyn Alden reports Fed balance sheet expansion at ~$40B/month through tax season — the "gradual print" has begun, which is structurally inflationary even as the Fed holds rates.

### The Dollar: Structural Weakness Consensus Among Independents

The single strongest consensus among contrarian voices — unanimous bearish on the USD:
- **Bridgewater/Dalio:** "Capital wars" — European holders could dump U.S. assets. The old virtuous cycle (run deficits, foreigners recycle into Treasuries) is breaking down.
- **Lyn Alden:** "Fiscal dominance" — deficits so large the Fed must eventually monetize, eroding the currency.
- **Gromen:** Tariff policy is "functionally indistinguishable from saying we want gold to be the neutral reserve asset." Dollar must weaken for the debt math to work.
- **Druckenmiller:** "Foreigners are way, way overloaded in dollars" — bearish at historic purchasing power peak.
- **BIS:** Observes capital rotation away from USD assets into European and EM equities already underway.

This matters for your portfolio because 92.9% of your exposure is USD-denominated. The contrarian consensus supports both your gold positioning (as a USD hedge) and the institutional case for adding non-US equities.

## 3. Asset Class Views

### US Equities: SPLIT — Consensus Fracturing

The most divisive asset class. The S&P 500 at 6,507 (NTM P/E ~18x) sits at the fault line between "earnings are still growing 12%" and "valuations are priced for perfection in a supply shock."

**Bulls:** Goldman (12% total return, S&P target ~7,600) and Morgan Stanley (S&P target 7,800) argue earnings growth remains intact, AI capex is real, and the correction is a buying opportunity. Wilson sees another 5-7% downside before an April low, then resumption of the bull market.

**Bears:** GMO forecasts -6.0% real annual returns over 7 years — the worst of any major asset class. AQR notes the narrowest risk premium of any major equity market. Vanguard warns that AI creates economic upside but stock market downside long-term. BofA FMS shows fund managers at net 17% underweight US equities.

**The smart disagreement:** Is this an early-cycle dip (Morgan Stanley) or a late-cycle distribution under a supply shock (everyone else)? The answer likely depends on how long the Iran war lasts and whether oil stays above $100.

### International DM: Mild Overweight — Rotation In Progress

- BofA FMS: Eurozone net 21% OW (down from 35%), Japan net 14% OW (swung from UW).
- GMO's highest-conviction idea is international deep value (+8.2% real/year forecast).
- Goldman sees dollar weakness as a tailwind for non-US returns.
- BlackRock favors Japan (corporate governance reforms) but is selective on Europe.
- Morgan Stanley is the dissenter: prefers US, expects only +4% for MSCI Europe vs. +14% S&P.

### EM Equities: Strong Overweight — Broadest Consensus

The clearest consensus call across all sources. BofA FMS shows net 53% overweight — highest since February 2021. Every firm with a stated view (BlackRock, JPM, Goldman, GMO, AQR) is overweight.

Drivers: attractive valuations, higher earnings growth, AI hardware supply chain (Korea/Taiwan), commodity exposure, improving governance. The dollar weakening supports EM returns.

### Government Bonds / Duration: Split on Duration

- **5-10 year:** PIMCO's highest conviction — overweight intermediate duration. JPM and Vanguard agree. Starting yields of ~4.3% on the 10-year provide income and potential capital gains if the economy weakens.
- **30-year:** PIMCO explicitly underweight. BlackRock says long-term Treasuries no longer provide portfolio ballast — the 60/40 paradigm is broken in a supply-shock world.
- BofA FMS: still net 36% underweight bonds overall, but slowly normalizing.

### Credit: Overweight With Quality Bias

Goldman, JPM, and Morgan Stanley (HY) are constructive. Corporate fundamentals are strong, maturity walls extended, defaults low. But spreads are historically tight — PIMCO warns the skew is toward wider spreads. BlackRock prefers private credit and infrastructure over public IG.

**The systemic risk:** 63% of BofA FMS respondents say private credit is the most likely source of a credit event. Credit default risk indicator at highest since April 2025.

### Commodities: Overweight — Stagflation Hedge

BofA FMS at net 34% overweight — highest since April 2022. Oil at $112 driving the allocation. But JPM and Goldman do NOT see a commodity supercycle — JPM expects an eventual oil supply glut, Goldman says copper/steel need synchronized global manufacturing growth that isn't present.

Energy (XLE) is the tactical play. Industrial metals (COPX) are more contested — lack the demand-side catalyst without China construction recovery.

### Gold: Overweight But Crowded and Correcting

The most complex call this month. Gold corrected 10% from ~$5,000 to $4,514 in a week — the worst week in four decades.

**Bullish structural case (JPM $5,000 target, Goldman $5,400):** Central bank purchases (~60 tonnes/month), Western ETF inflows (~500 tonnes since early 2025), "debasement trade" by UHNW/family offices. Trends not exhausted.

**Crowding risk (BofA FMS):** "Long gold" tied as the most crowded trade (35% of respondents). 38% now say gold is overvalued. The correction may have further to run near-term.

**Contrarian reinforcement:** Gromen and Lyn Alden provide the structural thesis underpinning the institutional gold call. Gromen argues gold is being functionally repositioned as the "neutral reserve asset" in a post-dollar-hegemony world. Druckenmiller is long gold as a geopolitical hedge.

**BIS warning:** Gold and silver in early 2026 "traded more like risk assets than safe havens" — the correction was driven by leveraged position unwinds, not fundamental selling. This suggests gold's safe-haven premium is not durable and can be overwhelmed by margin calls.

**Net read:** Structurally bullish (JPM, GS, Gromen, Lyn Alden, Druckenmiller all aligned), tactically vulnerable (BofA crowding + BIS leverage unwind risk). The correction is a buying opportunity IF the structural thesis (de-dollarization, reserve diversification) remains intact — which the weight of evidence says it does.

### Crypto: Consolidating

Bitcoin at ~$70,000, down from highs. No major institutional house has a stated view in their current research. BofA FMS doesn't track it. Morgan Stanley notes crypto has corrected alongside speculative equities since September 2025.

### Cash: Rebuilding

Every firm says underweight cash strategically — but tactically, cash is being rebuilt. BofA FMS cash at 4.3% is a significant shift. In a stagflationary environment with positive real cash yields (Fed at 3.5-3.75% vs. CPI at 2.4%), holding cash is no longer punitive.

## 5. Thematic Spotlight

### Theme 1: The Stagflation Trade Is On

For the first time since 2022, stagflation is the dominant macro framework. BofA FMS shows 51% of managers pricing below-trend growth + above-trend inflation. The rotation is clear: out of US equities and growth, into commodities, gold, EM, and cash.

This matters because stagflation environments are historically the worst for traditional 60/40 portfolios. BlackRock explicitly says the 60/40 paradigm is broken. PIMCO and Vanguard still believe bonds provide diversification, but only at intermediate durations — not long-end.

**Contrarian counter-argument:** Morgan Stanley says this is NOT stagflation — it's an early-cycle correction. Wilson argues the "three-year rolling recession ended" and the Iran disruption is temporary. If oil falls back below $90 on a ceasefire, the entire stagflation narrative unwinds rapidly. The AAII bearish sentiment at 46.4% and CNN Fear & Greed at "Extreme Fear" are historically contrarian bullish signals.

### Theme 2: The Great Rotation Away From US Exceptionalism

The BofA FMS captured something structural: fund managers are rotating out of US equities at the fastest pace on record, into EM (53% OW — 5-year high), Japan, and commodities. GMO's 7-year forecast of -6% real annual returns for US equities vs. +8.2% for international deep value is the analytical foundation.

Goldman's structural gold call ($5,400 target) and the "debasement trade" thesis reinforces this — the rotation is not just about equity valuations but about the entire US-dollar-denominated financial system being repriced.

**Contrarian counter-argument:** US earnings growth remains 12%+. AI capex is real spending generating real revenues. The S&P has delivered four straight years of gains. The US private sector is healthier than any other region. Betting against US equities has been a losing trade for 15 years.
