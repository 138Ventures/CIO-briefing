1 Executive Summary

March 2026 marks a regime shift. The Iran war has triggered the first genuine supply-shock since 2022, pushing Brent crude above $112 and forcing the Fed into a hawkish hold at 3.50-3.75%. The rate-cut narrative that sustained markets through 2024-2025 is effectively dead — June cut probability has collapsed to 18.4% and even December sits at just 60.5%.

Investor sentiment has collapsed at record speed. The BofA Fund Manager Survey recorded the largest single-month drop in growth expectations in its history, with 51% of managers now pricing stagflation. Cash levels surged to 4.3% from 3.4% — the biggest monthly jump since March 2020. The CNN Fear & Greed Index sits at "Extreme Fear" and AAII bearish sentiment hit 46.4% (vs. 31% historical average).

The S&P 500 broke below its 200-day moving average at 6,507 — its fourth straight week of declines. Gold corrected 10% from ~$5,000 to $4,514 in a single week, the worst weekly drop in four decades, despite being the consensus overweight trade. The BIS noted gold "traded more like a risk asset than safe haven" as leveraged positions unwound.

The institutional consensus has shifted to: stagflation risk is real, US equities are vulnerable, and the rotation trade is into EM equities, commodities, and gold. Morgan Stanley stands nearly alone as the major bull, calling this an early-cycle buying opportunity with a 7,800 S&P target.

However, BofA's Global Wave indicator complicates the picture. The proprietary 7-component global cycle model has signaled a trough and is rising — historically a bullish signal (median 16.5% ACWI return over subsequent 12 months, 92% hit rate). This does not negate the oil shock but suggests the underlying economic cycle may be stronger than sentiment implies.

Positioning is not yet at capitulation. BofA Bull & Bear at 8.4 (still a "sell" signal), Global Breadth Rule at -9% (buy signal requires -88%). Markets need to fall another 3-5% to trigger a contrarian buy. This is NOT a March 2020 or Liberation Day washout.

TacSignal confirms: The quantitative tactical model (as of March 18) aligns with the institutional consensus — EM, Japan, Asia-Pac, gold, and oil all show OW signals. US equities are neutral, not overweight. The agreement between institutional views and systematic signals strengthens conviction on the rotation trade.

Key Takeaway From Price Targets

Goldman and Morgan Stanley both see 17-20% upside in the S&P 500 — but this requires earnings growth to persist through a supply shock, which is the core disagreement with bears. Gold targets ($5,000-5,400) imply 11-20% upside from current corrected levels. The most striking data point: Goldman's Q4 2026 base case for Brent is $71 (vs. $112 today) — meaning oil is priced for a war premium that Goldman expects to fade by 37%. If they're right, the stagflation narrative unwinds. If they're wrong and oil stays above $100, everything changes.

Bitcoin is the widest-range forecast: $65K (Fidelity bearish) to $250K (Hoskinson). At $70K today, the institutional consensus median (~$150K) implies over 100% upside — but with no near-term catalyst identified and extreme sentiment bearishness.

2 Macro Regime

Growth: Decelerating Under Supply-Shock Pressure

The macro picture flipped in March. What was a consensus "soft landing / no landing" narrative three months ago is now a stagflation debate. The Iran war — with Brent crude above $110 and Pentagon reports of ground troop preparations — has injected genuine supply-shock uncertainty into an economy that was already wrestling with tariff-driven inflation.

Goldman Sachs still forecasts 2.7% US GDP for 2026, but this predates the March oil spike. PIMCO had already flagged below-trend growth from tariffs and immigration tightening. JPM assigns a 35% recession probability. The BofA FMS shows 51% of managers now expect below-trend growth with above-trend inflation — the textbook definition of stagflation. Morgan Stanley’s Mike Wilson is the outlier, arguing this is early-cycle re-acceleration and the "three-year rolling recession" has ended.

Dissent — BofA Global Wave (March 18)

The Global Wave — an amalgam of 7 components across 50 countries — continues to improve despite the conflict. Five of seven components are positive: Global Industrial Confidence, Consumer Confidence, Capacity Utilization, Unemployment, and Producer Prices. Only Credit Spreads (widening) and Earnings Revision Ratio (declining) detract.

After previous trough signals, MSCI ACWI returned a median 16.5% over 12 months (92% hit rate). Best-performing regions post-trough: EM (18.5%) and Europe (18.2%). Best sectors: Semiconductors (42.5%), Diversified Financials (31.8%). Best styles: Momentum and Risk. The rising Global Wave suggests the global cycle is more synchronized than in recent years — a meaningful counterweight to the FMS sentiment collapse.

Inflation: Re-Accelerating From Energy Shock

February CPI held at 2.4%, but the February PPI surged to 3.4% — the hottest since February 2025. Core PPI rose 0.5% MoM vs. 0.3% expected. EY estimates March CPI could spike to 3.3% as gasoline prices jump ~15%. The BofA FMS inflation expectations indicator surged from net +9% to net +45% in a single month — the most violent repricing of inflation expectations since 2022. PIMCO flags tariff passthrough as an additional inflation risk layered on top of energy.

BofA tracking Feb core PCE at 0.40% m/m (3.0% y/y). Headline PCE also at 0.40% m/m (2.8% y/y). The Fed estimates a 10% WTI increase adds ~7bp to core inflation — sticky, slow-moving pass-through. Shipping costs, natural gas, fertilizers, helium, and aluminum are also surging, raising risks of a broader supply disruption akin to 2021-22.

Central Bank: Hawkish Hold

The Fed held at 3.50-3.75% on March 18. The dot plot still pencils in one cut for 2026, but with "elevated upside risks to inflation." Critically, the neutral rate was revised up to 3.0% — meaning current rates aren't seen as particularly restrictive. CME FedWatch shows June cut probability at just 18.4%, September at 43.6%, December at 60.5%. Markets now price ~25bps of total cuts for 2026.

Hike Scenario Now on the Table (BofA Bhave, March 20)

Markets have nearly priced out cuts. BofA identifies three necessary conditions for hikes: (1) unemployment stays below 4.5%, (2) core PCE rises above 3.2% (currently 3.0%), and (3) Powell remains Chair (Warsh would be more dovish). The "sweet spot" for hikes: WTI averaging $80-100/bbl for 2026 — a sustained but moderate shock where inflation concerns outweigh labor market fears. Paradoxically, a larger shock makes hikes less likely (demand destruction caps inflation). If hikes materialize, BofA estimates ~75bp of tightening. Sen. Tillis blocking Warsh's confirmation extends the window.

The ECB held at 2.0% on March 19 and raised its 2026 inflation forecast to 2.6% while cutting its growth forecast to 0.9%. Lagarde replaced her prior "good place" framing with crisis-management language. Markets are now pricing potential ECB hikes — a complete reversal from Q4 2025. Stephen Miran dissented at the FOMC in favor of a cut, and Kevin Warsh (Trump's nominated successor to Powell, whose term ends May 2026) has signaled a preference for lower rates — adding policy uncertainty.

Liquidity: Tightening With a Hidden Twist

Cash levels are surging (BofA FMS: 4.3%, largest monthly jump since March 2020). The dollar is strengthening (DXY 99.4), which tightens global dollar liquidity. Credit conditions are deteriorating: 46% of FMS respondents say default risks are above normal (up from 17%), and 63% identify private credit as the most likely source of a systemic credit event — the highest proportion ever recorded. The BIS flags Synthetic Risk Transfers (SRTs) — bank balance-sheet risk offloaded to non-banks has grown fivefold since 2016, covering ~EUR 800B in loans. This is opaque, fragmented, and a systemic risk to monitor.

But there's a counterforce: Lyn Alden reports the Fed has begun balance sheet expansion at ~$40B/month through tax season, with a structural baseline of $20-25B/month thereafter. She calls this the "gradual print" — the path of least resistance as tax receipts fail to close the fiscal gap. This is structurally inflationary even as the Fed holds rates.

The Dollar: Structural Weakness — Strongest Contrarian Consensus

The single strongest consensus among independent voices is unanimously bearish on the USD:

  • Bridgewater/Dalio: "Capital wars" — European holders could dump U.S. assets en masse. The old virtuous cycle (run deficits, foreigners recycle into Treasuries) is breaking down. Dalio in Feb 2026: "We are quite close to [capital war], and it would be very easy to go over the brink."
  • Lyn Alden: "Fiscal dominance" — deficits so large the Fed must eventually monetize, eroding the currency.
  • Luke Gromen: Tariff policy is "functionally indistinguishable from saying we want gold to be the neutral reserve asset." Dollar must weaken for the debt math to work.
  • Druckenmiller: "Foreigners are way, way overloaded in dollars" — bearish at historic purchasing power peak. He calls this the best macro opportunity set in 10-15 years.
  • BIS: Observes capital rotation away from USD assets into European and EM equities already underway.

Your portfolio: 92.9% USD-denominated. This is your biggest structural vulnerability. The contrarian consensus supports both your gold positioning (as a USD hedge) and the institutional case for adding non-US equities.

3 Asset Class Views

US Equities: SPLIT — Consensus Fracturing

The most divisive asset class this month. The S&P 500 at 6,507 (NTM P/E ~18x) sits at the fault line between "earnings are still growing 12%" and "valuations are priced for perfection in a supply shock."

Bulls: Goldman (12% total return, target ~7,600) and Morgan Stanley (target 7,800) argue earnings growth remains intact, AI capex is real spending generating real revenues, and the correction is a buying opportunity. Wilson sees another 5-7% downside before an April low, then resumption of the bull market. He recommends Financials, Consumer Discretionary, and Small/Mid-caps.

Bears: GMO forecasts -6.0% real annual returns over 7 years — the worst of any major asset class. AQR notes the narrowest risk premium of any major equity market. Vanguard warns "AI exuberance" creates economic upside but stock market downside long-term, with high earnings expectations already priced in. Hussman identifies this as the extended peak of "the third great speculative bubble in U.S. history" and warns of a "trap door" scenario — abrupt, cascading dislocation when risk-aversion meets a hypervalued market. BofA FMS shows fund managers at net 17% underweight.

The smart disagreement: Is this an early-cycle dip (Morgan Stanley) or a late-cycle distribution under a supply shock (everyone else)? The answer likely depends on how long the Iran war lasts and whether oil stays above $100. TacSignal is neutral on US equities (composite -0.53), not supporting the OW case.

BofA equity strategy nuance (Subramanian, March 16): Don't buy the index — buy within it. Energy and Consumer Staples are the value plays. De-escalation is priced into the S&P, higher-for-longer oil is not. Sideline cash ($8tn in money markets, mostly retiree-held) is bullish but won't flow into TMT dips — retirees want dividends and inflation protection, favoring Large Cap Value. Institutional cash at 5-year lows means rotation within equities requires selling existing holdings. Additionally, $1tn in TMT private equity is slated to IPO, ending the "equity shrinkage" narrative.

Semiconductor fundamental check (BofA Arya, March 18): Management meetings at NVDA, AVGO, AMD, INTC, AMAT, CDNS showed uniformly positive tone on multi-year AI capex. NVDA and AVGO have best near-term AI visibility. Memory cycle durable through CY27E (Micron PO raised to $500, 5-year supply contracts being signed). Global Wave data: semis are historically the best-performing sector post-trough (42.5% median 12m return). BofA's US Regime Indicator has entered "Recovery" — Value and Beta lead in this phase.

International DM: Mild Overweight — Rotation In Progress

GMO's highest-conviction idea is international deep value at +8.2% real annual returns — vs. -6% for US equities. BofA FMS: Eurozone net 21% OW, Japan swung from UW to net 14% OW. Goldman sees dollar weakness as a tailwind. BlackRock favors Japan for corporate governance reforms. Morgan Stanley dissents, expecting only +4% for MSCI Europe vs. +14% for the S&P.

TacSignal: Japan +1.53 (OW), Europe +0.27 (N). The quantitative model supports Japan but is more cautious on Europe. This aligns with BlackRock's selective view.

EM Equities: Strong Overweight — Broadest Consensus

The clearest consensus call across all sources. BofA FMS: net 53% overweight — highest since February 2021 and +1.6 standard deviations above historical average. Every firm with a stated view (BlackRock, JPM, Goldman, PIMCO, GMO, AQR) is overweight. Drivers: attractive valuations, higher earnings growth, AI hardware supply chain (Korea/Taiwan), commodity exposure, improving governance, and dollar weakness supporting returns.

TacSignal: EM +1.57 (OW), Asia-Pac +1.63 (OW). The quantitative model strongly confirms the institutional consensus. This is the highest-conviction cross-validated call in the entire outlook.

Government Bonds: Split on Duration

5-10 year: PIMCO's highest conviction — overweight intermediate duration. JPM and Vanguard agree. Starting yields of 4.37% on the 10-year provide meaningful income and potential capital gains if growth weakens. PIMCO describes this as a "rare opportunity to increase quality and liquidity without giving up equity-like return potential."

30-year: PIMCO explicitly underweight. BlackRock says long-term Treasuries no longer provide portfolio ballast in a supply-shock world — the 60/40 paradigm is broken. Gromen argues the Treasury market can only function if the Fed eventually monetizes, making long bonds a poor inflation hedge.

TacSignal: US Bonds -0.06 (N). The model is flat — no directional conviction either way.

Gold: Overweight But Crowded and Correcting

The most complex call this month. Gold corrected 10% from ~$5,000 to $4,514 in a week — the worst week in four decades.

Structural bull case: JPM ($5,000 target) and Goldman ($5,400) cite central bank purchases (~60 tonnes/month), Western ETF inflows (~500 tonnes since early 2025), and the "debasement trade" by UHNW/family offices. Gromen argues gold is being functionally repositioned as the "neutral reserve asset" in a post-dollar-hegemony world. Druckenmiller is long gold as a geopolitical hedge.

Crowding risk: BofA FMS — "long gold" tied as the most crowded trade (35% of respondents). 38% now say gold is overvalued. The BIS warned gold "traded more like a risk asset than safe haven" — the correction was driven by leveraged position unwinds, not fundamental selling.

Net read: Structurally bullish (JPM, GS, Gromen, Lyn Alden, Druckenmiller all aligned), tactically vulnerable (crowding + leverage unwind risk). The correction is a buying opportunity IF the de-dollarization thesis holds — which the weight of evidence says it does.

TacSignal: Gold +1.74 (OW). Quantitative momentum confirms the structural bullish case despite the tactical correction.

5 Thematic Spotlight

The Stagflation Trade Is On

For the first time since 2022, stagflation is the dominant macro framework. BofA FMS shows 51% of managers pricing below-trend growth + above-trend inflation. The rotation is decisive: out of US equities and growth, into commodities, gold, EM, and cash. BlackRock explicitly says the 60/40 paradigm is broken in a supply-shock world. PIMCO and Vanguard still believe bonds provide diversification, but only at intermediate durations — not long-end.

Contrarian counter: Morgan Stanley says this is NOT stagflation — it's an early-cycle correction. If oil falls below $90 on a ceasefire, the entire narrative unwinds rapidly. AAII bearish sentiment at 46.4% and CNN Fear & Greed at "Extreme Fear" are historically contrarian bullish signals.

The Great Rotation From US Exceptionalism

Fund managers are rotating out of US equities at the fastest pace on record, into EM (53% OW — 5-year high), Japan, and commodities. GMO's 7-year forecast: US -6% real/yr vs. international deep value +8.2%. The rotation extends beyond equities — Goldman's gold call ($5,400) and the debasement trade thesis mean the entire USD-denominated financial system is being repriced. Druckenmiller has rotated his portfolio away from AI into financials (XLF), copper, gold — and calls this the best macro opportunity set in 10-15 years.

Contrarian counter: US earnings growth is 12%+. AI capex is real spending generating real revenues. The US private sector is healthier than any other region. Betting against US equities has been a losing trade for 15 years.
